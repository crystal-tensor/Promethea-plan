# Benchmark Specifications v0.1

Last updated: 2026-06-13

This file defines the first benchmark layer for the initial four attack
directions. The goal is to make each research direction falsifiable: every
claim should eventually point to a dataset, circuit family, noise model,
baseline, metric, and acceptance threshold.

## Global Benchmark Rules

- All benchmark inputs must be stored or regenerated by deterministic scripts.
- Every run must record tool versions, random seeds, hardware target, noise
  model, and wall-clock environment.
- Every improvement claim should compare against at least two baselines when
  possible: a standard open-source tool and a published or widely accepted
  method.
- A claimed improvement must report both best case and median case across a
  suite, not only a hand-picked circuit.
- Negative results are valid outputs if the benchmark is reproducible and the
  failure mode is clearly explained.

## B1. Hardware-Aware Quantum Circuit Compression

**Problem ID:** 25

**Research claim to test:** A constrained synthesis and rewrite loop can reduce
hardware-weighted circuit cost without changing unitary behavior or measured
output statistics.

**Input families:**

- QASMBench OpenQASM circuits covering arithmetic, chemistry, simulation,
  search, and QFT-style routines.
- MQT Bench circuits across abstraction levels, using 2-130 qubit families
  where exact or statistical verification is still possible.
- Hand-built QEC syndrome-extraction circuits in Stim or a compatible
  stabilizer format.
- Small chemistry ansatz circuits generated through OpenFermion/Cirq or
  Qiskit-style pipelines.

**Target hardware models:**

- Linear nearest-neighbor.
- Heavy-hex-like sparse connectivity.
- All-to-all ion-trap-like connectivity.
- Neutral-atom-like reconfigurable connectivity.

**Baselines:**

- Native transpiler optimization at levels equivalent to light, medium, and
  aggressive.
- A peephole optimizer.
- A routing-aware compiler baseline.

**Metrics:**

- Logical depth.
- Two-qubit gate count and two-qubit depth.
- T-count and T-depth for fault-tolerant targets.
- Hardware-weighted error exposure:
  `sum(gate_count[type] * error_rate[type]) + idle_time * idle_error`.
- Compile time.
- Equivalence check pass rate.

**Acceptance threshold for first paper-quality result:**

- Median 20% reduction in hardware-weighted cost across at least 30 circuits,
  with no equivalence failures and compile-time overhead below 10x baseline.

**Failure conditions:**

- Gains only appear on circuits already optimized trivially by baseline tools.
- Equivalence is not proven or statistically validated.
- The method requires target-specific manual tuning per circuit.

## B2. Low-Overhead Quantum Error Correction

**Problem ID:** 22

**Research claim to test:** A searched code family plus decoder can lower
logical error per physical qubit or reduce qubit-time overhead against a
surface-code baseline under at least one realistic noise regime.

**Input families:**

- Surface-code memory experiments.
- Repetition-code and small color-code controls.
- Candidate quantum LDPC or subsystem-code Tanner graphs.
- Hardware-constrained syndrome-extraction schedules.

**Simulation tools:**

- Stim for stabilizer circuit generation and sampling.
- A matching decoder baseline.
- A neural or learned decoder only if paired with a transparent fallback
  decoder for validation.

**Noise models:**

- Depolarizing circuit-level noise.
- Biased dephasing noise.
- Measurement error.
- Leakage proxy model if tooling supports it.
- Correlated two-qubit error stress test.

**Metrics:**

- Logical error rate per round.
- Threshold estimate with confidence interval.
- Space-time volume per target logical error.
- Decoder runtime per shot.
- Robustness under noise-model mismatch.

**Acceptance threshold for first paper-quality result:**

- At least one regime where space-time overhead improves by 25% or more over a
  tuned surface-code baseline at the same target logical error, with confidence
  intervals and reproducible seeds.

**Failure conditions:**

- Decoder improves only on the exact noise model used for training.
- Candidate code has impractical measurement schedule or connectivity.
- Runtime scales too poorly to support useful distances.

## B3. Molecular Reaction Dynamics Resource Estimation

**Problem ID:** 49

**Research claim to test:** Observable-first quantum algorithms can estimate
reaction-relevant quantities with lower resources than full state preparation
or full wavefunction reconstruction.

**Candidate systems:**

- Small transition-metal active-site model.
- Nitrogen-fixation-inspired FeMo-cofactor fragment or reduced proxy.
- Photochemical conical-intersection proxy.
- Standard small molecules for calibration: H2, LiH, H2O, N2, and small
  organic reactions.

**Tooling:**

- OpenFermion for fermionic Hamiltonians and qubit mappings.
- Classical chemistry backends such as PySCF where available.
- Resource estimator for T-count, logical qubits, circuit depth, and precision.

**Baselines:**

- Phase estimation on full Hamiltonian.
- VQE-style ansatz resource estimate.
- Classical coupled-cluster, selected CI, DMRG, or tensor-network reference
  where applicable.

**Metrics:**

- Target observable error, in chemical accuracy units when appropriate.
- Logical qubits.
- T-count and T-depth.
- State-preparation assumptions.
- Classical preprocessing cost.
- Sensitivity to basis size and active-space choice.

**Acceptance threshold for first paper-quality result:**

- A resource estimate showing a credible path to at least 5x lower logical
  depth or T-count for one observable-focused task compared with a full
  Hamiltonian phase-estimation baseline, without hiding cost in state
  preparation.

**Failure conditions:**

- Claimed gain depends on unrealistic QRAM or perfect state preparation.
- Classical baselines are outdated or weak.
- The chosen molecule is too small to illuminate the hard-regime behavior.

## B4. Verifiable Quantum Advantage Protocols

**Problem ID:** 16

**Research claim to test:** Task distributions can be designed so that useful
quantum computations carry embedded checks, enabling confidence without full
classical reproduction.

**Task families:**

- Random circuit sampling with trap subcircuits.
- IQP or commuting-circuit variants with hidden structure.
- Hamiltonian simulation instances with conserved quantities or checkable
  projections.
- Cross-device consistency tasks where two devices answer linked challenges.

**Baselines and adversaries:**

- Honest noisy quantum simulator.
- Tensor-network classical simulator where feasible.
- Spoofing strategies: marginal matching, low-depth surrogate circuits,
  generative output models, and trap-aware adaptive adversary.

**Metrics:**

- Completeness: honest device pass probability.
- Soundness: adversary pass probability.
- Sample complexity.
- Classical verification time.
- Robustness to hardware noise.
- Usefulness of the task beyond pure randomness certification.

**Acceptance threshold for first paper-quality result:**

- A protocol with explicit completeness/soundness estimates and empirical
  evidence that at least two spoofing families fail at benchmark sizes where
  full distribution verification is unavailable.

**Failure conditions:**

- Embedded checks make the task classically easy.
- Verification cost scales like full classical simulation.
- Noise tolerance is too low for near-term or early fault-tolerant devices.
